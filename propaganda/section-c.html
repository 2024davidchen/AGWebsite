Section C

Section C is mainly focused on adding random things that make the speaker's arguement seem better but are actually kind of irrelevant

Note: For this section especially, make sure to watch out for No Techniques. The technique only applies if the details given are irrelevant. If the details are actually relevant (they make sense in the context) then it is most likely No Technique

Most of these techniques can also be used either positively or negatively

Techniques:

  1. Appearance
  2. Manner
  3. Degrees and Titles
  4. Numbers
  5. Status
  6. Repetition
  7. Slogans
  8. Technical Jargon
  9. Sophistical Formula

Definitions:

  1. Appearance

    The speaker uses appearance to make their opinion

    Examples:

    • Look at the way Bruno dresses! He must be pretty smart too. (1 Appearance)
    • Look at the way Bruno dresses! We should hire him as a model. (0 No Technique)
  2. Manner

    The speaker uses actions or behavior to judge

    Examples:

    • Briar is too nervous and jittery. I don't think we should hire him as our new dishwasher. (2 Manner)
    • Briar speaks and acts with confidence. They'll be a great addition to our theater club. (0 No Technique)
  3. Degrees and Titles

    The speaker uses a degree or title that demands respect (like Ph.D, Lord, Doctor, Professor, etc.) to strengthen their arguement when it doesn't actually make sense.

    Examples:

    • I heard former President Obama went to Zingerman's. That must be a really great resturaunt. (3 Degrees and Titles)
    • Professional food critique Hal Houmeyer endorsed Zingerman's. Zingerman's must be a really good resturaunt. (0 No Technique)
  4. Numbers

    The speaker uses numbers (usually really high or low) to emphasize a point

    • 56% of the people who take the AP Chinese exam get a 5. It must be really easy.
  5. Status

    The speaker uses celebrity or anyone with fame to try to support their arguement

    Examples:

    • I heard Obama went to Zingerman's. That must be a really great resturaunt. (5 Status)
    • Why would anyone style their hair like that? It makes them look like Donald Trump. (5 Status)
  6. Repetition

    The speaker repeats a word to emphasize it

    Examples:

    • Ad for Little Caesars: Our pizza has the most cheese, most pepperoni, and most customers. Come get one of our Extra Most Bestest pizzas today.
  7. Slogans

    The speaker uses a slogan (short and catchy phrase) to appeal to the customer

    Examples:

    • Instead of buying a home console, buy one of our custom Nexus portable game consoles. Same price. Built Different.
  8. Technical Jargon

    The speaker uses complex words or terms to make their arguement seem more impressive

    Note: This one mostly relies on the audience. If the audience is expected to know the term, then it should be 0 No Technique.

    Examples:

    • Ad: Our Nexus gaming consoles come with an 12th gen i9 CPU, an RTX 3080, and 16 gigabytes of RAM. Buy one today. (8 Technical Jargon)
    • Video Game Critique: Although the new Nexus gaming console seems appealing with its powerful specs, the 10000 mAh battery makes it a lot more limited than you think. (0 No Technique)
  9. Sophistical Formula

    The speaker uses an old saying to support their arguement, but doesn't really use any logical reasoning other than the saying

    Examples:

    • It's not a good idea to only study Presidents. Afterall, "don't put all your eggs in one basket". (9 Sophistical Formula)
    • I don't understand why you don't put any work into AG. Even if you did well during the Saturday Tournaments, that doesn't guarentee that you'll do well at States. It's better if you just put some time and effort into AG. Afterall, it's better safe than sorry. (0 No Technique)
