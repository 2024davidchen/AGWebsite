Section C

Section C is mainly focused on adding random things that make the speaker's arguement seem better but are actually kind of irrelevant

Note: For this section especially, make sure to watch out for No Techniques. The technique only applies if the details given are irrelevant. If the details are actually relevant (they make sense in the context) then it is most likely No Technique

Examples:

  • Look at the way Melissa is dressed for her interview. Clearly she fits our qualifications for a software developer. (1 Appearance)
  • Look at the way Melissa is dressed. We should hire her as a model. (0 No Technique)

Techniques:

  1. Appearance
  2. Manner
  3. Degrees and Titles
  4. Numbers
  5. Status
  6. Repetition
  7. Slogans
  8. Technical Jargon
  9. Sophistical Formula

Definitions:

  1. Appearance

  2. Metaphor and Simile

    The speaker compares/describes something symbolically, not literally. A metaphor is worded in a way that makes it seem almost literally, while simile is worded using "like" or "as"

    Examples:

    • Coach to kids: "Kids, please pay attention. I know learning Ling is hard, but it is a mountain you have to overcome." (Metaphor)
    • Don't invite George into our friend group, he's like a rat; small, hard to deal with, and he'll bite you the first change he gets." (Simile)
  3. Emphasis

    The speaker emphasizes what somebody else has said in a way that takes it a bit too literally or exact to change the meaning

    Examples:

    • Ian's mother is furious after he didn't wash the dishes when she told him to last night

      Mother: "I told you I wanted you to wash the dishes last night"

      Son: "You said you wanted me to, not that I had to"

    • A man gets pulled over for drinking and driving

      Man: "But officer, I wasn't drinking while I was driving. Therefore, I was only driving.

  4. Quotation Out of Context

    The speaker quotes something but cuts it out in a way that changes the meaning to their use

    NOTE FOR HIGH SCHOOL ONLY: You are expected to know a few famous quotes/sayings, and if the speaker modifies them for their own use, it is still considered Quotation Out of Context

    • Sammy: "You could say our project was a success, if you ignore everything that went wrong."

      Allan: "Sammy said their project was a success."

  5. Abstract Terms

    The speaker uses a word or phrase but doesn't exactly know what it means. Usually the speaker will either try to avoid giving an actual definition when asked to or gives a definition that doesn't really mean anything

    Examples:

    • David: "I wish games today were a bit more old-school"

      August: "What do you mean by that?"

      David: "C'mon August, you should know what I mean by old-school"

  6. Vagueness

    The speaker makes something vague that isn't very specific, which brings multiple possible answers

    Examples:

    • Mother to son: "I know you have a math test tomorrow, but I don't want to see you staying up too late."
  7. Ambiguity

    The speaker words things things in a way that has 2 different meanings, even if it is clear that only one of the meanings makes sense

    Examples:

    • Mr. Torgue: "I probably shoulda set you up with a sponsor beforehand but I was busy suplexing a shark wearing a bolo tie when I should have been setting up sponsors." (Quote taken from Borderlands 2)
  8. Shift of Meaning

    The speaker uses a word multiple times but uses a different definition/meaning of it each time

    Homonyms (words that sound the same) are also considered Shift of Meaning

    Examples:

    • Everybody makes mistakes. But you drive yourself to be better. That's why you should drive a Honda today.
  9. Sophistical Formula

    The speaker uses a word multiple times but uses a different definition/meaning of it each time

    Homonyms (words that sound the same) are also considered Shift of Meaning

    Examples:

    • Everybody makes mistakes. But you drive yourself to be better. That's why you should drive a Honda today.
