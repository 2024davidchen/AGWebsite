Section E

Section E is mainly focused on choosing examples to strengthen the speaker's arguement

Techniques:

  1. Concurrency
  2. Post Hoc
  3. Selected Instances
  4. Hasty Generalization
  5. Faulty Analogy
  6. Division
  7. Composition
  8. Non Sequitor

Definitions:

  1. Concurrency

    The speaker states that because one thing happened during something else, one must have caused the other

    Simplified: Because A happened during B, B must have caused A

    Examples:

    • Biden was president when all the gas prices went up. It must be his fault that gas is so expensive.
  2. Post Hoc

    The speaker states because one thing happened after another, one must have caused the other

    Simplified: Because A happened after B, B must have caused A

    Examples:

    • Ever since Tara helped me get that hamster at the arcade, I've been getting a lot of kills in Overwatch. We should go to the arcade again and get something else so that I can do even better.
  3. Selected Instances

    The speaker chooses a few niche (rare) cases and uses them as if all cases are like this

    Examples:

    • I don't need to study Prop. David and Andrew didn't study and they did terrific.
  4. Hasty Generalization

    The speaker sees one thing happen and assumes that the future will be similar

    • Coach David tried to teach us Ling last week, but I didn't understand a thing. He must not be a very good coach.

  6. Vagueness

    The speaker makes something vague that isn't very specific, which brings multiple possible answers

    Examples:

    • Mother to son: "I know you have a math test tomorrow, but I don't want to see you staying up too late."
  7. Ambiguity

    The speaker words things things in a way that has 2 different meanings, even if it is clear that only one of the meanings makes sense

    Examples:

    • Mr. Torgue: "I probably shoulda set you up with a sponsor beforehand but I was busy suplexing a shark wearing a bolo tie when I should have been setting up sponsors." (Quote taken from Borderlands 2)
  8. Shift of Meaning

    The speaker uses a word multiple times but uses a different definition/meaning of it each time

    Homonyms (words that sound the same) are also considered Shift of Meaning

    Examples:

    • Everybody makes mistakes. But you drive yourself to be better. That's why you should drive a Honda today.
