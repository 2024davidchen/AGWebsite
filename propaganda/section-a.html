Section A

Section A is mainly about how the speaker thinks, and how this affects their judgement and choices

Techniques:

  1. Prejudice
  2. Academic Detachment
  3. Drawing the Line
  4. Not Drawing the Line
  5. Conservatism, Radicalism, Moderatism
  6. Rationalization
  7. Wishful Thinking
  8. Tabloid Thinking
  9. Causal Oversimplification
  10. Inconceivability

Definitions:

  1. Prejudice

    Think of prejudice as "pre-judging" something. This means that the speaker unfairly judging without knowing enough information or context

    Examples:

    • Everybody knows Asians are good at math. Angela's Asian so she must be able to help us study for our math test tomorrow.
  2. Academic Detachment

    Academic Detachment is where the speaker is given a decision but refuses to make one

    • The PS5 looks cool, but I like Xbox controllers better. I guess I won't buy either console.
  3. Drawing the Line

    The speaker is given only two choices when in reality, there are more possibilities

    • Mother to son: "It's 11 PM right now, you can either go study for your math test tomorrow or go to sleep"
  4. Not Drawing the Line

    The speaker does not set a limit where one should be made

    • I've already eaten 6 slices of pizza, I might as well finish the rest.
  5. Conservatism, Radicalism, Moderatism

    This technique is a combination of three concepts, if you see any of these, just select 5 and it will be considered correct

    • Conservatism

      Old is good (think about conserving something)

    • Radicalism

      New is good

    • Moderatism

      Compromise is good

  6. Rationalization

    Making Excuses

  7. Wishful Thinking

    Because the speaker wants it to be true, they think it will happen

  8. Tabloid Thinking

    The speaker bases their thoughts on common stereotypes

    Very similar to prejudice, the main difference is that tabloid thinking does not have an action associated with it

  9. Causal Oversimplification

    The speaker narrows the cause of a complex topic to only one or two reasons instead of the full piccture

  10. Inconceivability

    The speaker says something isn't true because they can't believe it's true (conceive means to understand or believe, inconceivability literally means you can't believe it)
